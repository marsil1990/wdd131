Our Story

Nacional was foundes in 1899 and is one of the most successful and historic football clubs in Uruguay and South America. With many local and international titles, the team has a rich heritage

The first years of the history

1899 - The Birth of Club Nacional de Football

On May 14, 1899, 22 young men aged between 13 and 20 gathered at Ernesto Caprario's house to found Club Nacional de Football—the first creole club in the Americas and the oldest football club in Uruguay. During the founding session, they established the club's principles, name, and kit. Sebastián Puppo was elected as President, and Domingo Prat as Captain.

1899 - The First Match in History

On June 25, 1899, Nacional defeated Uruguay Athletic's second team 2-0 in Punta Carretas, on a field loaned to them by Juan Cat, Manager of the Sociedad Comercial de Montevideo. Melitón Romero scored the first-ever goal in Nacional's history, followed by Juan Carlos Vallarino.

1899 - The First Fan and the First Supporters

Miguel Prudencio Reyes, who handled equipment and inflated the balls, cheered Nacional loudly from the sidelines. His constant encouragement during games led spectators to ask, “Who is that?” — “That's the fan... the ball inflator of Nacional.” The word *hincha* (fan) was born at Club Nacional de Football.

1900 - Nacional Arrives at Gran Parque Central

On May 27, 1900, Nacional played its first match at the newly inaugurated Gran Parque Central against Deutscher F.K. Located on the historic grounds of the “Quinta de la Paraguaya,” where José Artigas was proclaimed leader of the Orientals in 1811, the stadium became Nacional’s home. It is the oldest stadium in the Americas and the 16th oldest in the world.

1902 - The White Jersey and the Club Flag

On March 24, 1902, Miguel Nebel proposed replacing the red and blue shirt with a white one—a motion that was approved. On May 23, the club's official flag was created. Domingo Prat suggested the colors blue, white, and deep red. Ernesto Caprario presented a sketch: a blue flag with a white diagonal stripe and the red inscription “C. N. de F.”
